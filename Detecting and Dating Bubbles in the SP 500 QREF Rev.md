Highlights: 
Cointegration tests controlling for skewness and excess kurtosis on price and dividend data from the S&P 500 show evidence of periodically collapsing bubbles.
Application of a rolling windows test (GSADF) for explosive behavior also shows evidence of bubbles. 
The GSADF test also dates bubbles.  Episodes around the Spanish Flu in 1917 and the housing crisis in 2008 were detected for every relevant choice of sample and series.

Abstract:  Two robust tests on S&P 500 data show evidence of bubbles.  A test of stationarity controlling for skewness and excess kurtosis is designed to detect periodically collapsing rational bubbles.  Test for cointegration and direct tests on the price-dividend and price-earnings ratios cannot reject non-stationarity for all the samples considered.  A test for explosive behavior on rolling window of the data also provide evidence of multiple bubbles and allow for dating of bubbles using the same set of variables.  The two bubble episodes detected in all relevant cases are associated with the Spanish Flu in 1917 and the housing crisis in 2008. 
Keywords: SADF, GSADF, cointegration, stock market bubble, periodically collapsing bubbles

I. Introduction
Identifying bubbles, when stock prices deviate from fundamentals, is important for both investors and policymakers but is econometrically difficult. Proper determination of the null and the alternative hypothesis requires an assumption about the type of bubble being detected and a choice of econometric features to detect.  The present work focuses on the Periodically Collapsing Rational Bubble (PCRB) model of Evans (1991) for data from the S&P 500 stock market.
This study revolves around two of the most reliable tests for bubbles, the Taylor and Peel (1998) residuals augmented Dickey-Fuller (RADF) test to determine stationarity and the Phillips, Shi and Yu (2015) generalized supremum augmented Dickey-Fuller (GSADF) test for explosive behavior.  The latter test is capable of dating bubble episodes as well. The tests are conducted on four primary series, the price-dividend ratio, the price-earnings ratio, and the residuals from cointegration regressions of dividends and earnings on the price.
The evidence for the presence of bubbles is overwhelming, though the dating of bubble episodes depends on the choice of test variable and sample.  The periods that show bubble behavior for all the tests are associated with the Spanish Flu and Post World War I in 1917 and the financial crisis in 2008 that started in the housing market.
Taylor and Peel (1998) show that testing for cointegration with standard unit root tests is biased in the presence of PCRBs, and they develop a test that controls for the potential skewness and excess kurtosis. The Phillips, Shi and Yu (2015) test is based on the work of Phillips, Wu and Yu (2011) who develop a test for explosive behavior based on ADF tests for a rolling window of samples.  Phillips, Shi and Yu (2015) show that this sup ADF (SADF) technique only works well when there is single bubble in the series. The generalized sup ADF (GSADF) test is an extension of SADF test which is more effective for multiple periodically collapsing bubbles. Instead of fixing starting point at the first observation in the full data, GSADF employs flexible starting and ending points. Homm and Breitung (2012) discuss the SADF test in the context of other related econometric approaches.
Our goal is to provide a complete set of results using reliable techniques on updated S&P 500 data with a number of plausible variables.  The techniques are the RADF test of Taylor and Peel (1998) and the GSADF test of Phillips et al. (2015). The variables include the price-dividend ratio, the price-earnings ratio and cointegration residuals for both pairs.  This paper extends the sample, which is desirable since most studies do not include the end of the Great Recession collapse.  
II. Literature Review
There are a number of earlier studies that focus on the S&P 500 data through the 1980s using cointegration and unit root tests for bubbles.  The results of those papers either reject the presence of bubbles, such as Taylor and Peel (1998) or Campbell and Shiller (1987), or give mixed results.  Froot and Obstfeld (1991) find evidence of a non-linear relationship between price and dividends but interpreting that as evidence of a bubble has difficulties.  Diba and Grossman (1988) use a cointegration test and find mixed results.  Craine (1993) reports similar results for cointegration tests that allow for a time-varying discount factor.  Cunado, Alana and Gracia (2005) estimate fraction unit root processes to detect deviations from long run equilibrium in price and dividend data for NASDAQ, whereas the cointegration approaches described above restrict attention to orders of integration of one or zero.
There are alternatives approaches to test for bubbles. Hall, Psaradakis and Sola (1999) use a regime shifting approach to identify changes between stationary and integrated behavior.  They identify deviations from long run equilibrium for prices and dividends but state that bubbles are not the cause.  A related approach in Busetti and Taylor (2004), which is discussed in Homm and Breitung (2012), uses a Chow test to detect such a switch.  Their Monte Carlo simulations suggest that the modified test of Busetti and Taylor (2004), and Chow test outperform the SADF test of Phillips, Wu and Yu (2011) if there is one crash and when the crash occurs at the end of sample. However, it does not hold when there are multiple crashes.
Phillips, Shi and Yu (2015) introduce the rolling GSADF tests that allow for multiple bubbles, building on the test SADF test for single bubbles in Phillips, Yu and Wu (2011), and show evidence for a number of bubbles episodes in the sample 1871-2010. The present paper uses the same test for extended sample and selection of variables.  Our results largely agree, but some bubble episodes that arise for Phillips, Shi and Yu (2015) are not apparent for all the tests in the present paper, the technology bubble around 2000 being a prominent example.
      Bala (2016) also applies the GSADF technology to analyze the price-dividend ratio for a smaller sample and identifies several bubbles: 1974, Black Monday 1986-1988, the dotcom bubble 1996-2001, and the housing crisis bubble 2008-2009. 
The SADF test has been applied to agricultural commodity markets by Gutierrez (2011) finding bubble episodes.  Bohl, Kaufman and Stephan (2013) applies SADF test and the regime shifting test of Hall, Psaradakis and Sola (1999) on daily and weekly data of ÖkoDAX and the DAX subsector Renewable Energies. Their tests exhibit speculative bubbles in both series and identify starting and ending points.  Zhenya at al. (2016) reports the SADF and GSADF tests on monthly data of Shanghai price-dividend ratio. The SADF test identifies one bubble whereas the GSADF tests identifies three periodically collapsing bubbles between 2000-2015.
This work focuses on the PCRB model of Evans (1991), which is most common for empirical work, though other bubble models exist.  The original rational bubble model is that of Blanchard and Watson (1982).  Parke and Waters (2014) and Waters (2018) develop a model where agents switch between forecasting strategies, including a rational bubble forecast.
Other bubble models focus on learning key parameters.  Lansing (2010) describes how agents update a key parameter in the intrinsic bubble model of Froot and Obstfeld (1991) to generate observed levels of asset price volatility.  Adam, Marcet and Niccolini (2016) develop a consumption-based asset pricing model where the subjective estimate of the discount factor is updated according to a learning mechanism.  The model can match a number of empirical features of the S&P 500 data.

2.  Sup augmented Dickey–Fuller (SADF) tests
Another approach is to test for the explosive behavior produced by a rational bubble. Phillips, Wu and Yu (2011) suggests the Sup augmented Dickey-Fuller (SADF) test that uses recursive regression techniques to test the unit root against the alternative right-tailed explosive hypothesis. By simulation studies, Phillips, Wu and Yu (2011) demonstrate the ability of the SADF test in detecting periodically collapsing bubbles.  The GSADF test introduced by Phillips, Shi, and Yu (2015) is based on the SADF test but allows for multiple bubble episodes. Another advantage of these tests is that they allow for dating of the bubble episodes.
Both tests are different than the cointegration tests in the previous section in that they test against an alternative hypothesis of explosive behavior, which is motivated by the bubble term (3.3), where the null is a unit root, rather than testing for any variety of non-stationarity. 3.  Generalized Sup Augmented Dickey-Fuller (GSADF) Test

Figure 4 confirms the conclusion of the GSADF tests for bubbles in that there is much stronger evidence for collapse around the housing crisis in 2008 than there is for the commonly discussed tech bubble of 2000. One natural interpretation is that there was a bubble around 2000, but it did not spread beyond technology stocks.  Hence, bubble tests on for the NASDAQ, as in Phillips, Wu and Yu (2001) do give evidence of bubbles, but the results on the S&P 500 presented here are less conclusive. 
Though the data is limited at this time, the price indices show a very modest effect around COVID, indicating the market participants think the disruptions are temporary, and may even benefit the housing and technology sectors.
VI.  Conclusion 
The most reliable tests cannot reject periodically collapsing rational bubble in the S&P 500 stock index.  Tests for non-stationarity and tests that detect explosive behavior on the price-dividend ratio, the price-earnings ratio and the associated cointegration residuals give similar qualitative results, though the dating of the bubbles can vary.  Two events are notable since they are detected by all the relevant tests, the Spanish Flu of 1917 and the housing bubble and crash around 2008.  
Preliminary GSADF tests on the most recent data indicate one period of explosive behavior in December 2021.  However, the fact that the results changed from the sample ending in 2010 in the Phillip, Wu and Yu (2015) paper and the present work indicate that using the test for real time bubble detection should be done with trepidation.
Standard tests for stationarity are not capable of detecting periodically collapsing bubbles, which explains the results in Table 2-3, but the RADF test of Taylor and Peel (1998) that controls for the skewness and excess kurtosis that could arise does give evidence of bubbles in every series and sample tested.  Similarly, the SADF test rejects explosive behavior, with the exception of the price-earnings ratio, but the GSADF test that allows for multiple episodes does give evidence of bubbles and also allows for dating of the episodes.
These results should give pause to investors and policymakers alike.  The price data does not always reflect fundamentals and should be interpreted with great caution.  The results here identify periods when markets are efficient and when they are not, which should help our understanding of the connection between prices and fundamentals so decisions based on future market behavior can be made with more confidence.
